Creating the Next Generation Mentorship Experience

FORMATIVE & EVALUATIVE UX RESEARCH | WEB DESIGN & DEVELOPMENT

1.  THE CHALLENGE

Project Access is a nonprofit organization that helps socioeconomically disadvantaged students apply to international top universities in the UK, US, and Europe. They used a variety of different technologies and platforms to deliver their services to students.  However, the fragmented services across many different platforms added a lot of friction to the process and led to a bad user experience. They lost a lot of potential applicants because the application process had been non-transparent and long-winded and there were too many services. In order to explore efficient ways to deliver user-centric online learning, I performed research into mentorship platforms and how to best incentivize flourishing peer-to-peer mentorship. 

My Role

UX Researcher

Objective

Create a platform that combines these services in a single place in a user-friendly manner

Teams

Project Manager
UX Designer
Web Developer 

2.  PROJECT TIMELINE

 Week 0: Meet the Teams + UX Research Plan

I was grateful and excited to be selected to work on a very unique product with Project Access Austria after rounds of assessments. 

To kick off the project, I met three teams: the program organizer Menon Labs to get to know the context and the community, the startup partner Project Access Austria to understand product requirements and expectations, and finally my own awesome product team with whom I would be working collaboratively to build a website in the coming weeks. 

It’s very helpful to understand the project focus and expected deliverables, and it’s no less important to know your teammates, their responsibilities, technical skills and personalities, especially when the team is small, and timeline is tight. Together, we read and examined the product brief and discussed the following questions on our first meeting: 

•  What’s the user journey across the different services?
•  What’s the functionality that the product must deliver at each of these stages?
•  Why would a user first sign up on the website?
•  What are the data points that Project Access Austria should collect on their users?
•  How can the platform be connected to the pre-existing Project Access database?

It was critical to learn as much as we could and narrow down the scope before diving into the user research. From there, we defined each’s role and responsibilities and I made a plan for UX research. This way, my progress would be visible and manageable to myself and my teammates could more efficiently plan their work and ensure we check and follow closely each other’s progress. 

 Week 1 Competitive Analysis

In order to determine strategies that would provide us with a distinct advantage, and any weaknesses that could be exploited within the product development cycle, I and the product designer reviewed several popular platforms through the lens of users and functionalities and analyzed strengths and weaknesses of them. 

 Week 2: User Interview + Open Card Sorting 

In order to better understand international students’ application processes and what good mentorship is about, I decided to talk to some real users. However, I was unexpectedly told that we weren’t able to have access to past users. As a compromise, I mobilized all my teammates and encouraged them to find their international friends and relatives that would match our target users’ profiles as closely as possible. At the current stage, we did not know whether levels of education would have an impact on the applicant experience, so we needed to hear voices from both undergraduate applicants and post-graduate applicants. In the end, each member managed to schedule and interview two international participants within 3 days. Proud of my team! 

Since I learnt from the competitive analysis that the information architecture of mentorship platforms is prone to confusion, in addition to the typical interview questions, I decided to also design a card sorting experiment to discover how users conceptualize information and generate insights about the naming convention and content strategy of the marketing website. 

Because some of my teammates had never had any experience with user interviews and none of them had experience with the card sorting, I wrote detailed research guide and walked them through before we started any interviews.

In the research process, we used Google Survey to automatically organize interview responses into a worksheet and adopted Optimal Workshop to implement the card sorting task.

 Week3 Results Synthesize: Affinity Mapping + User Journey + Sitemap

After talking with international applicants, I hosted a workshop for affinity mapping and invited the designer and the product manager so that everyone got a chance to contribute their insights, empathize with our users and we were able to brainstorm and prioritize solutions together.

We synthesized our research findings by laying out what our participants did, thought, frustrated about, and needed during application processes. This way, we started to see repeated patterns in user behaviors and attitudes and then we regrouped similar notes, which led us to key user pain points and unmet needs. Interestingly, though we originally distinguished undergrad and postgrad applicants, looking into the interview results, we found they were quite similar in terms of their application journey. Therefore, I mapped out the most common applicant journey to make the experience more intuitive and identified room for improvement. 

Next, based on the sorting results and session notes from open card sorting studies, I suggested a sitemap for the marketing website to lay out the relationships between contents and place contents at where users expect to find them. 

Associating the previous research results, I emphasized on three things:

 1. Simplicity.
Even with a small number of participants, we got many different ways to go about the categorization and that’s perhaps why most of the mentorship platforms were found to have unclear structures. Thus, I wanted to keep the site as simple as possible by only highlighting necessary pages before users logging in. 

 2. Sense-making.
During card sorting sessions, one of the most commonly asked questions is” What is the mentee/mentor?”. When no context is given, users will need to use a lot of imagination to fill the gap in the meaning, which might lead to misunderstandings. Therefore, to avoid these situations, I made “About the Program” the homepage so that users could quickly pick up the context they need to understand the rest of the website. In a similar manner, “Webinars” and “Forums” alone can be very confusing but when put under “Academy”, users will come around that they are educational. 

 3. Value.
As the main purpose of the marketing website is to provide value for users or prove we can create future value, I consciously included contents that would align with potential end-user value. For instance, I expected to include mentors introduction, program impact, past application results with data and successful stories of applicants under “About the Program”; common ranking websites and other useful school & program information under “Resources”. 

As we were already half-way into the project, I presented all the above findings to the whole team while revisiting the initial competitive analysis. I also delivered actionable recommendations of how to move forward. The presentation was well received and directly guided the product development.

 Week 4-5 Progress Checking (MVP) + Prototype Test + Synthesize 

Due to time constraints, we had to move relatively quickly and set priority. To make sure that we were on track and prioritized right features, we had a midpoint meeting with our startup partner Project Access and came out of it with detailed MVP requirements guiding our next steps. 

While I was researching about users, the designer simultaneously started designing prototypes and made modifications as insights came in. Till this point, we already had working low-fi prototypes, which the web developer would soon implement and build out a functional site based on it. 

Before refining details, I conducted a quick prototype test to validate 1) if the design communicates what the website is at first glance 2) if users understand what each part does 3) if the design measures up to their expectations, etc.

3.  REFLECTIONS

Research with limited time and resources

I learned and grew the most when it came to designing and executing research plans adaptively with limited time and resources. Under these circumstances, methods selection is no longer calculated in an ideal world that ignores friction. I am more aware of the trade-off between the difficulty/cost of acquiring users and the quality of responses, the feasibility/cost of a research method and the potential bias it would have.

Be ready to wear multiple hats

As a team member in a startup, sometimes I did non-typical UX Research work —— a little bit design, a little bit development and even video editing! The experience of wearing multiple hats equipped me with not only skills that not all researchers would have, but also the empathy to understand my collaborators’ work.
